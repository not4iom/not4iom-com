The proposal

What is actually being proposed

The Isle of Man is defined by its natural horizons, its quiet seas, its dark skies and its unique wildlife. Here is what 87 turbines off our east coast would mean.

Who is behind it? A Danish development, for UK benefit, in Manx waters. The Mooir Vannin Offshore Wind Farm is proposed by Ørsted, a Danish energy multinational, through Mooir Vannin Ltd — their special-purpose company, registered here in the Isle of Man and named after the Manx words for our own sea. The planning application is now under examination, with Ørsted hoping for consent to proceed. NOT4IOM says: NO to Mooir Vannin.

87 industrial turbines

An industrial power station built across the sea off our east coast — not a handful of turbines, but dozens upon dozens.

From just 6 miles out

The turbine array begins just 6 miles offshore — close enough to dominate the view from our coast and change our seascape forever.

81 square miles of our waters

A vast footprint covering much of the sea that defines the island's east coast.

Over prime fishing grounds

Built across some of the island's most important Manx fishing grounds — including key queenie (queen scallop) beds — with years of construction disruption.

The power is for the UK

From the outset, always intended for the UK grid — with no guarantee of supply, security or cheaper power for us.

An unclear clean-up bill

If the operator is gone in 25–35 years — or sells the company on — the island could face a potential £400M+ decommissioning bill, and no fund yet protects future generations from it.

Taller than anything we know

Each turbine would stand up to 350m — higher than the world's most famous structures.

Why we must stand together

The reality behind the marketing

First proposed back in 2015, this project has had no transparent, meaningful public engagement since. The statutory MIMA pre-examination consultation failed — because the public is only now beginning to understand what it really means.

Five concerns sit at the heart of this campaign. As more emerge, they'll be added to the campaign's View Information page.

No energy security

From the outset, the power from Mooir Vannin was always intended for the UK grid. The developer talks of “possible” direct supply to the Island — but there's no guarantee we'd receive any of the power, gain energy security, or see cheaper bills. Nor will it help us meet our own 2030 clean-energy targets.

Bringing any of it ashore is estimated to cost Manx taxpayers £120M+ in capital alone. If Mooir Vannin land the undersea connector cable at Groudle Bay, Manx Utilities would need to connect there, carry it across to Pulrose and upgrade our grid to handle 100MW — plus ongoing balancing costs. That money could build our own renewables, for real energy resilience.

"Is OUR island bearing the burden for foreign grid capacity?"

Minimal financial benefit

The benefit to the island is estimated at less than 1% of our GDP a year — and only payable when there's a profit. Mooir Vannin would first write down capital costs of around £4.5 billion before showing any profit, so when the Island would see meaningful revenue is anyone's guess. Seabed rental, meanwhile, has been minimal — about £10,000 a year for the last decade, with future payments unknown.

Key documents, including the 2015 Agreement for Lease, remain unpublished, and Ørsted's claimed £2 billion value doesn't match the Government's own 2020 report. And although Mooir Vannin is Manx-registered, the Treasury confirmed (June 2026) that the Island's current tax framework doesn't even allow these profits to be taxed here — the law would have to be changed first.

"Will OUR island heritage be betrayed for 30 pieces of silver?"

Environmental damage

We risk permanent damage to our landscapes, seascapes and dark skies — and our status as the world's only Whole Nation UNESCO Biosphere. Around 80% of our visitors come for exactly this.

Years of construction, operation and maintenance threaten our birds, bats, marine mammals, fish and the sea bed itself — and the fishing grounds beyond.

"Will we prostitute OUR island? For what?"

Industrialised waters & future expansion

This risks permanent loss of sea room for navigation and ferry routes. The East Irish Sea Transmission Project already refers to offshore "farms" — plural — and a Multi-Purpose Interconnector: infrastructure sized to serve many more installations than this one.

With no marine plan and an opaque lease, we risk ceding our waters for decades to uncontrolled, unconsulted expansion — unable to use our own sea for our own energy projects.

"Will OUR island become 'wind-farm island'?"

Decommissioning & the clean-up bill

One day, all 87 turbines — their foundations and cabling — must be removed. If the operator is gone in 25–35 years, or has sold the company on, who pays to clear our sea?

A Freedom of Information request confirmed the decommissioning “guarantor” is Ørsted's parent company — and, at worst, the Danish Government — not a ring-fenced fund here in the Isle of Man. And with Ørsted routinely divesting its wind farms, there's no guarantee the company that builds it will be the one left to dismantle it.

The clean-up could run into hundreds of millions — potentially £400M+. These funds must be ring-fenced now, so future generations of Manx people aren't left bearing the cost of removing the infrastructure at the end of its life.

"When it's all over — who pays to clear our sea?"
